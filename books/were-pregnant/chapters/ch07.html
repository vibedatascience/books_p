The Seventh Month

The third trimester begins: baby gains critical weight, brain development accelerates, and a premature birth at this stage carries a 96% survival rate.

Week 28 — A Jump in Viability

Week 28 marks a significant threshold. Babies born at this stage now have a 96 percent survival rate — a reassuring number, even as full-term delivery remains the goal. The baby weighs roughly 2.25 pounds (about the size of a small eggplant) and is likely rotating into a head-down position in preparation for birth. Lung maturation is underway, and the brain is producing neurons at a rapid pace.

For the mother, the growing baby is beginning to compress internal organs. Backaches and sciatic pain become more common as the baby’s weight presses on the sciatic nerve. Shortness of breath may occur as the uterus pushes upward against the lungs. Doctor’s appointments now shift to every two weeks, and the provider may recommend STD screening, since certain infections can cause complications at delivery. If earlier Rh testing was negative, immunoglobulin injections will begin.

This is a good time to finalize the nursery — assembling the crib, testing out a glider or rocking chair, and ensuring the basics are in place for the hospital bag, diaper bag, and car seat. A glider serves double duty: useful for nighttime feedings and soothing the baby back to sleep during those early weeks.

Week 29 — Smiling on the Inside

The baby has grown to roughly 15–16 inches and weighs between 2.5 and 3 pounds — about the size of a butternut squash. REM sleep cycles have begun, meaning the baby may now be dreaming. Bones are mineralizing (hardening), and the forehead is expanding to accommodate a rapidly growing brain. The baby’s current weight will more than double — possibly triple — over the remaining weeks.

Movements shift from forceful kicks to softer jabs and pokes as the baby runs out of room. The mother’s body is now producing prolactin in anticipation of lactation, and colostrum may begin leaking from the breasts. Urination has gone from frequent to near-constant as the uterus fills and presses on the bladder. Breast pads can help manage leakage during daily activities.

Interview a pediatrician this week if you haven’t already. Ask friends for referrals, research online, and schedule a brief meet-and-greet to see whether the practice aligns with your family’s values and approach. This is also a good window for enrolling in childbirth classes — Lamaze, newborn care, or infant CPR — if you haven’t done so yet.

Week 30 — Rapid Brain Development

At about 16 inches and close to 3 pounds, the baby’s brain is undergoing dramatic structural changes. Grooves and creases are forming across the brain’s surface, creating the folds that will support cognitive development from infancy through childhood. The brain has also taken over thermoregulation, so the fine lanugo hair that once kept the baby warm is gradually disappearing.

Other milestones this week: hands are fully developed and the baby is actively grasping objects. Bone marrow is now producing red blood cells. The baby may be screened for staph, and a “kick count” test may be used to monitor fetal health. Another ultrasound could be scheduled.

The mother’s ligaments are beginning to relax in preparation for delivery. Breast discomfort continues, and sleep deprivation is compounding exhaustion. Begin discussing a postpartum sleep plan now. Who handles nighttime feedings? If the mother is breastfeeding and pumping, will the partner take some overnight shifts? Without four to six hours of uninterrupted sleep, both parents will struggle. If neither can sacrifice sleep, consider hiring a night nurse or postpartum doula for the first few weeks.

Week 31 — Head Turning

The baby is approaching birth length and weighs about 3.5 pounds — roughly the size of a coconut — but still has another 3 to 5 pounds to gain before delivery. A trillion neural connections are forming: the baby is processing information, tracking light, and receiving signals from all five senses. It can now move its head from side to side. Sleep periods are growing longer, and the mother can often distinguish the baby’s waking hours from its resting periods.

Braxton Hicks contractions are increasing in frequency and intensity. Only the mother can learn to differentiate practice contractions from true labor — her instincts matter here. Sciatic pain remains common when the baby rests on or kicks the nerve, sending shooting pain from the lower back down through the legs.

Restless legs syndrome affects roughly 15 percent of pregnant women and can severely disrupt sleep. Acupuncture, yoga, and meditation have been reported to help ease the burning and tingling sensations. Some women also experience “lightning crotch” — an intense, sudden shooting pain deep in the pelvis or vagina, likely caused by the baby pressing on the nerve leading to the cervix.

Practical priorities this week: begin batch-cooking meals and freezing them in portioned bags. Homemade dishes that can be reheated in minutes will be invaluable during the early postpartum weeks when neither parent can see straight. Also consider babyproofing — walk through your home looking for open outlets, accessible household cleaners, and other hazards, then compare your list against a professional checklist.

The Bottom Line

“Month seven is about crossing the viability threshold and entering the home stretch. The baby’s brain is developing at a staggering pace, weight is climbing fast, and the body is preparing for life outside the womb. For parents, this is the time to finalize the nursery, choose a pediatrician, and start planning for the realities of postpartum life — because these last twelve weeks will go faster than you think.”
