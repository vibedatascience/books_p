The Sixth Month

The final month of the second trimester — the baby crosses the viability threshold, gains fat rapidly, and begins to open its eyes as the family prepares for the home stretch.

Weeks 23–27 Overview

Month six closes out the second trimester and brings one of the most significant milestones of the entire pregnancy: fetal viability. Starting around Week 24, a baby born prematurely has a meaningful chance of survival with intensive medical care — and that probability improves dramatically with each passing week. The baby is adding fat to fill out its loose skin, its lungs are maturing, and by the end of this month it will open its eyes, recognize voices, and display distinctly infant-like behaviors. For the pregnant partner, physical discomfort is intensifying — back pain, heartburn, swelling, and disrupted sleep are now constant companions — but mood generally remains stable.

Week 23 — Packing on the Pounds

The baby is about the size of a grapefruit — roughly 11 inches long and just over one pound. Blood vessels in the lungs are developing as the respiratory system prepares for life outside the womb. The skin is growing faster than the baby can fill it with fat, giving it a wrinkled, loose appearance that will smooth out over the coming weeks.

The growing baby is pushing the mother's liver upward and her belly outward. Water retention and swelling in the feet and legs may become noticeable, particularly in warm weather. Mood tends to remain even and upbeat despite increasing physical discomfort. Appetite is hearty, but eating smaller, more frequent meals can help manage the persistent flatulence, bloating, and heartburn. This is a good time to double-check the baby registry and finalize nursery purchases, including a baby monitor, crib bedding, and any remaining larger items.

Week 24 — Viability

This is a landmark week. The baby has reached the viability threshold: approximately 50 to 75 percent of babies born at 24 weeks survive with intensive neonatal care, and the survival rate increases with each additional day in the womb. The baby is now about a foot long — roughly the length of an ear of corn — and its lungs, though still immature, are formed. The face is fully developed, with eyelashes, eyebrows, and hair (though pigmentation has not yet appeared).

The uterus is now approximately the size of a soccer ball. The pregnant partner may be experiencing lower back pain, difficulty sleeping, persistent heartburn, and limb swelling. Encourage rest, hydration, and healthy eating habits. A glucose screening test for gestational diabetes is typically scheduled around this week. The test involves drinking a concentrated sugar solution and having blood drawn before and after. If results indicate gestational diabetes, the provider will work with the family to establish a dietary plan that maintains healthy glucose levels. This is also the right time to pack hospital bags — if the baby arrives early, you will want essentials ready to go.

Learn the early signs of pre-labor: a sudden drop in the baby's position, water breaking, severe lower back pain, regular contractions, or bloody vaginal discharge. If any of these appear, contact your provider immediately.

Week 25 — Losing Wrinkles

The baby weighs approximately 1.5 pounds and measures about 13 inches — roughly the size of an acorn squash. Fat accumulation is accelerating, and the formerly wrinkled skin is beginning to smooth out. Capillaries are forming under the skin and filling with blood. By the end of this week, air sacs lined with capillaries will develop in the lungs, a critical step toward independent breathing — though the lungs still need more time to mature fully. Hair is beginning to acquire pigment.

The growing uterus is putting significant pressure on blood vessels leading to the lower extremities, increasing swelling. Discomfort and disrupted sleep persist. If the pregnant partner is feeling unusually fatigued, ask the provider about iron-deficiency anemia — a common condition at this stage that can be addressed through dietary changes or supplementation. Iron-rich foods like sauteed spinach and lean red meat can help. Smaller, more frequent meals continue to be the best strategy for managing heartburn. This is also a good time to revisit the birth plan, including decisions about epidural use, and to begin researching doulas if that is part of your approach.

Week 26 — Eyes Open

The baby weighs close to two pounds and is about 14 inches long. After several weeks of having the eyelids fused to allow the retina to develop, the eyes are beginning to open. The iris does not yet have pigmentation, but it will develop in the coming weeks. Senses are sharpening, lungs are continuing to strengthen, and if the baby is a boy, the testicles have now descended into the fully formed scrotum.

The pregnant partner's center of gravity has shifted substantially, contributing to clumsiness and difficulty getting up from seated positions. Heartburn may intensify — often described as a strong burning sensation in the esophagus accompanied by shortness of breath. Combined with the growing discomfort of an expanding abdomen, this can significantly disrupt sleep and increase irritability. Braxton Hicks contractions continue as the body rehearses for labor. Understanding that these are normal "practice contractions" — not signs of actual labor — can help reduce anxiety when they occur.

Week 27 — End of the Second Trimester

The second trimester concludes with the baby displaying distinctly infant-like behavior: opening and closing its eyes, napping on a schedule, sucking fingers, getting hiccups, and beginning to recognize familiar voices. The baby has more taste buds now than it will have at birth, and many mothers notice distinct fetal reactions to specific foods.

Frequent bathroom trips, continued clumsiness and brain fog, and physical discomfort from leg cramps and back pain are standard at this stage. If the belly button has protruded, it is harmless — any piercings can remain in place unless they catch on clothing. For persistent itching from skin stretching, thicker moisturizers like coconut oil, shea butter, or belly butter are more effective than standard lotion. As the second trimester ends, make sure all major preparation milestones are accounted for: nursery, registry, hospital bags, birth plan, and — if desired — doula interviews.

The Bottom Line

“The sixth month transforms theoretical possibility into tangible reality: the baby is viable, visibly human, and behaviorally infant-like. The gravity of what is coming sharpens.”

“Physical discomfort is now the dominant theme for the pregnant partner. Prioritize rest, hydration, and small meals. Have hospital bags packed, the birth plan finalized, and all major logistics in place before the third trimester begins.”
