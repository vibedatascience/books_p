The Fourth Month

The second trimester begins — nausea fades, energy returns, and the baby bump becomes unmistakable as your baby grows from a baseball to a pomegranate.

Weeks 14–17 Overview

Month four marks the start of the second trimester, which many families regard as the most comfortable stretch of pregnancy. The nausea and dizziness that defined the first trimester typically begin to subside. Appetite and energy rebound. The belly shifts from looking like post-holiday bloating to a clearly recognizable baby bump. This is also a productive window for practical preparation: setting up the nursery, notifying employers, and beginning to think seriously about birth plans and baby registries.

Week 14 — Making Faces

The baby is now almost the size of a baseball, roughly 3.5 inches long. The body is lengthening, the neck has formed, and the kidneys are beginning to function. A fine layer of hair called lanugo is developing across the skin, which will help regulate body temperature in the womb. Morning sickness is fading for most, though acid reflux may persist or worsen as the uterus continues to expand. Second-trimester prenatal appointments begin now, typically scheduled every four weeks. Genetic testing may be offered at this visit, and if the pregnancy is considered high-risk, insurance generally covers the cost. Gender determination can also be included in the same panel.

This is a practical week for household logistics. If you have not already, begin planning the nursery — whether that means converting a guest room or reorganizing an existing space. The pregnant partner should avoid lifting or pushing heavy furniture from this point forward. It is also wise to inform your employer about the pregnancy and begin exploring maternity and paternity leave options, including short-term disability coverage that may be available in your state.

Week 15 — Baby Can Breathe

The baby's lungs, though still primitive, are now functioning at a basic level. The legs have grown longer than the arms, and taste buds are beginning to develop. At around 4 inches, your partner may feel the first fetal movement this week — often described as a flutter, a muscle spasm, or even a movement of intestinal gas. These early sensations are easy to miss, but they represent a significant developmental milestone.

Very early Braxton Hicks contractions may begin appearing — these are the body's way of preparing for childbirth and are generally painless. A common cognitive side effect also emerges around this time: so-called pregnancy brain, characterized by forgetfulness, mild brain fog, and occasional clumsiness. Psychological stress may also increase, including anxieties about fetal health. Stay alert for signs of prenatal mood disorders, which can affect both partners.

If genetic testing was not covered by insurance, an ultrasound may be scheduled this week to determine the baby's sex. This is also a good time to discuss a preliminary birth plan: home birth versus hospital, natural versus medicated, and contingency scenarios such as emergency cesarean delivery. Having these conversations early reduces decision-making pressure later.

Week 16 — Getting Ready for a Growth Spurt

The baby is now close to five inches long, with muscles growing noticeably stronger. The head is held more erect, and scalp patterns are forming though hair has not yet appeared. The baby can now respond to external stimuli — gentle pressure on the abdomen may produce a visible reaction. The uterus has expanded to roughly the size of a papaya.

For the pregnant partner, energy is returning and moods are stabilizing. Appetite has normalized or increased. Breasts continue to grow and may remain tender. This is an excellent time to create a baby registry if you have not started one. Focus on high-priority items: an infant car seat (ideally one that snaps into a coordinated stroller), a crib or convertible sleep setup, a baby monitor, bottles and feeding supplies, and a well-stocked first-aid kit. If you plan to have more than one child, consider gender-neutral colors for larger items so they can be reused. Note that car seats have expiration dates — always verify before accepting hand-me-downs.

Week 17 — A Little Baby Fat

The baby is now the size of a pomegranate and is accumulating subcutaneous fat at a rapid pace. Bones are hardening from cartilage, sweat glands are beginning to develop, and the umbilical cord is strengthening and thickening. A major milestone: the baby can now hear sounds, including voices from outside the womb.

The pregnant partner's belly is growing steadily, shifting her center of gravity and potentially increasing clumsiness. Blood flow is ramping up, which may cause the occasional bloody nose. As the skin stretches, itching may become noticeable across the body — fragrance-free coconut oil or similar moisturizers can help. Weight gain is accelerating compared to earlier weeks. If the first second-trimester appointment has not happened yet, schedule one this week. This is also a good time to sign up for childbirth preparation classes.

The Bottom Line

“Month four is a turning point — the discomfort of the first trimester eases, the baby's major systems are maturing rapidly, and there is a window of relative energy and optimism to tackle practical preparations.”

“Use this period to address logistics: nursery setup, employer conversations, registry building, and initial birth-plan discussions. The calm will not last forever.”
