The Eighth Month

Organs finish developing, the skull stays flexible for delivery, and final preparations — birth plan, hospital tour, car seat — shift from optional to urgent.

Week 32 — Fully Developed Organs

By week 32, every organ is fully developed except the lungs, which continue to practice inhaling and exhaling amniotic fluid in preparation for independent breathing. The baby weighs approximately 4 pounds and measures 15–17 inches — about the size of a spaghetti squash. Skin has finally become opaque, and fat continues to accumulate steadily.

For the mother, sleep is arriving in shorter and shorter bursts, making it harder to maintain focus and even moods. The belly button may be starting to push outward. The baby is pressing lower, increasing bladder pressure. Braxton Hicks contractions are strengthening, and breast leakage or vaginal discharge may occur. Sleeping with the head elevated can help counter heartburn, and a pregnancy wedge pillow can make leg positioning more comfortable.

Finalize the birth plan this week. Share it with your doctor and their team. If the mother becomes overwhelmed in the delivery room, her partner (and doula, if applicable) will need to serve as her advocate and spokesperson — guiding the medical team through the plan and speaking up with confidence if complications require deviation. This is also a good time to discuss cord blood banking if you’re considering it. Stay current on third-trimester test results; staph screening results, for instance, may influence breastfeeding decisions.

Week 33 — Flexible Skull

The baby is gaining roughly half a pound per week and is steadily preparing its digestive system for feeding through consistent intake of amniotic fluid. A critical structural feature is developing: the skull has not yet fully fused. The unfused area, called the fontanel (commonly known as the “soft spot”), provides the flexibility the baby needs to pass through the birth canal. The baby is likely in a head-down position and dropping toward the lower pelvis.

The mother is tired, uncomfortable, and eager for delivery. She may be constantly hungry because so many nutrients are going to the baby, yet eating is difficult because meals often cause heartburn and discomfort. Small, frequent, nutrient-dense meals and protein smoothies can help bridge the gap.

Learn to distinguish between urine leakage and amniotic fluid leakage. Urine is typically yellow with a faint ammonia smell. Amniotic fluid is pale, clear, and has a slightly sweeter odor. If there is any doubt, contact your provider immediately. Confirm that you are registered with the hospital or birthing center, and schedule a tour of the labor and delivery unit and postpartum recovery rooms. Familiarizing yourself with the layout — emergency room entrance, parking, floor number — reduces stress when the real moment arrives.

Week 34 — Vernix and Final Lung Development

The baby is 17–18 inches long and approaching 5 pounds — about the size of a cantaloupe. Fingernails have reached the fingertips. The lungs are in their final week of development. For boys, the testicles have typically completed their descent into the scrotum; about 3–4 percent of boys are born with undescended testicles, which usually resolve before the first birthday and are not cause for alarm.

The mother’s uterus is now 500 to 1,000 times its pre-pregnancy size. She likely cannot see below her waist and may be experiencing significant anxiety as delivery approaches — amplified by the many birth stories well-meaning friends have been sharing. Hydration is critical even though frequent urination makes it feel counterproductive. Do a dry run to the hospital this week. Know the primary and alternate routes, where to park, and whether you need a parking ticket at the gate.

Install the car seat now. Consult the vehicle handbook for the approved installation process. Many brands have online video guides; take the time to get this right rather than scrambling at the last minute.

Week 35 — A Big Milestone

The lungs are now fully developed. If born this week, the baby has a strong chance of breathing independently. The kidneys, liver, and all other organs are also fully functional. The baby is roughly 18 inches long and just over 5 pounds. From here, the remaining weeks are primarily about weight gain and continued brain development. If the baby hasn’t already flipped head-down, it is likely doing so now.

The mother may experience shortness of breath if the baby hasn’t yet dropped, or increased bladder pressure if it has. Braxton Hicks contractions are more frequent and more easily mistaken for real labor. This is the last week of biweekly appointments — from here on, visits are weekly. The baby can come at any time now.

Practical priorities: pack the hospital bag (this is a final warning — early arrivals happen). Finalize a labor-day logistics plan that covers household duties — who feeds the pets, turns on lights so the home doesn’t look vacant, and notifies family. Understand the three stages of labor: the first stage involves contractions that dilate the cervix; the second stage is pushing and delivery; the third stage is delivery of the placenta.

The Bottom Line

“Month eight is when preparation shifts from planning to execution. The baby’s organs are completing development one by one, lungs reaching full maturity at week 35. For parents, this is the month to finalize the birth plan, tour the hospital, install the car seat, and pack the bag — because from here on, the baby can arrive at any time.”
