The Twelfth Month

Weeks 51–54: The final stretch of the fourth trimester — baby sleeps through the night, personality emerges, and the family finds its new normal.

Week 51 — Settling In

By the start of the third month, most parents feel noticeably more confident. Baby is animated and interactive — smiling, gurgling, laughing, and engaging with the world. Baby may begin sleeping through the night and is developing the ability to self-soothe, whether through a pacifier, thumb-sucking, or simply settling back down after a brief waking.

During tummy time, baby is gaining enough strength to move from side to back. While rolling over fully may still be a few weeks away, the increased mobility means closer supervision during floor time is essential. Never leave baby unattended on elevated surfaces.

Mom should be feeling significantly better — more rested, more confident, and past the worst of the hormonal fog. "Pregnancy brain" has given way to "baby brain" — a tendency toward distraction driven by the constant demands of newborn care rather than by hormonal shifts. If mom continues to breastfeed exclusively, it can help with weight management and may keep her period at bay. Any over-the-counter medication should still be cleared by a physician while breastfeeding. Generally only about 1 percent of a medication passes into breast milk, but doctors rightly err on the side of caution.

Week 52 — Established Routines

At 10 weeks old, baby has a recognizable daily rhythm: wake, feed, play, nap, repeat. Naps last roughly an hour each, with two to three per day. Baby is eating four to six ounces every three to four hours, with larger amounts during growth spurts. Total sleep is 15 to 17 hours per day, with the bulk concentrated in an 8- to 10-hour overnight stretch.

Mom's physical recovery is now primarily a function of sleep quality, diet, exercise, and whether she is still breastfeeding. It is common for mothers to feel occasional guilt about not savoring every moment — this is normal and does not reflect poor parenting. Both parents should actively create opportunities for the other to step away, decompress, and reconnect with their own identity outside of caregiving.

If baby has mastered tummy time, you can begin gently encouraging rolling over during floor play. Support the motion rather than forcing it — baby will get there on their own timeline.

Week 53 — Emerging Personality

Baby's personality is increasingly visible. You will recognize specific preferences — favorite toys, preferred positions, particular sounds that elicit laughter. Cooing, smiling, and giggling are now regular features of daily life. Baby is still gaining about seven ounces per week and may be outgrowing the first round of clothing.

This is the beginning of oral exploration. Baby will attempt to put anything within reach into their mouth. Begin scanning your environment for choking hazards — small objects, loose coins, pet toys, button batteries. This is an early step in the babyproofing process that will intensify as baby becomes mobile.

Mom may experience a wave of nostalgia as the newborn phase starts to recede. Hair shedding — sometimes dramatic — may continue but will normalize. As baby nears three months and mom approaches her pre-pregnancy weight, new wardrobe needs (for both baby and mom) are common and worth planning for.

Week 54 — The Three-Month Mark

Three months ago, this baby did not exist in your daily life. Now they are the center of it. Baby is eating five to seven ounces per sitting, on a schedule of four to five feedings per day. From this point forward, expect weight gain of about two pounds per month through the first seven months. Developmental milestones will accelerate rapidly.

If mom is returning to work after an extended leave, expect a range of emotions — guilt, relief, anxiety about childcare, and separation concerns. All of these feelings are normal. She will need to decide whether to continue pumping at work, transition to formula, or combine the two. There is no single correct answer; the best plan is the one that is sustainable for your family.

Begin planning for the next phase: discuss solid food introduction (typically at four to six months) and food-allergy prevention strategies with your pediatrician. Baby's next checkup and round of immunizations will be at the four-month mark. Now is also the time to begin serious babyproofing — corner bumpers on tables, outlet covers, cabinet locks, toilet-lid locks — because a crawling baby arrives faster than most parents expect.

Three-Month Milestones

Sleep, feeding, and weight benchmarks at the end of the fourth trimester

Source: Adrian Kulp, We're Pregnant! (2018) — as cited in We're Pregnant!

  • 1
    By three months, baby sleeps 15–17 hours per day with 8–10 hours at night. Self-soothing ability is developing — if baby wakes briefly, a few minutes of fussing may be all they need to resettle.
  • 2
    Feeding has consolidated to 4–6 ounces every 3–4 hours, roughly 6 times per day. Baby is approaching 15 pounds and gaining about two pounds per month.
  • 3
    Oral exploration begins now. Scan every room for choking hazards and start babyproofing well before baby becomes mobile. Electrical outlets, small objects, and cleaning supplies are top priorities.
  • 4
    Discuss solid food introduction (typically at 4–6 months) and food-allergy prevention with your pediatrician. Baby's next checkup and immunization round is at the four-month mark.
  • 5
    Both parents should take active steps to reconnect as a couple. If baby is accepting bottles and you have a trusted caregiver, a low-key local date night is a meaningful investment in the partnership.
The Bottom Line

“The fourth trimester ends with a family transformed. Baby is sleeping through the night, eating on a schedule, and developing a personality that is unmistakably their own. The hardest part of the newborn phase is behind you — what lies ahead is watching this person become who they are going to be.”
