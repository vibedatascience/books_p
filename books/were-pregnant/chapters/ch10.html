The Tenth Month

Weeks 43–46: The fourth trimester begins — birth recovery, newborn basics, and the sleep-deprived first month at home.

Week 43 — The Birth Week

The wait is over. Whether delivery was vaginal or surgical, the first days are a whirlwind of recovery, feeding attempts, and emotional recalibration. Most hospital stays last two to four days, during which nursing staff will walk you through diaper changes, swaddling, and initial breastfeeding. Many hospitals now favor "rooming in," keeping baby in the room with both parents rather than sending the newborn to a nursery.

In the first week, expect the baby to lose about 5 percent of birth weight — this is entirely normal and not cause for alarm. Baby will begin learning to latch onto the breast for feeding. If latching proves difficult, a lactation consultant (often available on-site at the hospital) can provide guidance. Breast milk at this stage contains colostrum, rich in antibodies that strengthen the newborn's developing immune system.

Baby's first bowel movements will be meconium — a thick, black, tar-like substance that is normal and expected. The umbilical cord stump, held by a small plastic clamp, will begin drying out. Exercise caution during diaper changes around the cord area; it can be sensitive until it naturally detaches in one to two weeks. If your baby boy was circumcised, follow the specific wound-care instructions provided by your medical team.

Mom's body is undergoing significant hormonal shifts. Her breasts are filling with milk and may be tender; if nursing, sore or chapped nipples are common. She will experience lochia — a heavy, period-like discharge that is a normal part of postpartum recovery. Approximately 80 percent of new mothers experience the "baby blues" — mood swings, tearfulness, and emotional volatility driven by rapid hormonal changes. In most cases this resolves within two weeks, but it requires attention and support.

Postpartum mood disorders are not limited to mothers. Fathers and partners can also experience depression, anxiety, and mood disruption due to sleep deprivation, lifestyle upheaval, and stress. Enlist a trusted friend or family member to check in on both parents regularly during the early weeks. Postpartum Support International maintains resources specifically for dads and partners.

Baby will need a pediatric checkup within the first week. Your pediatrician will likely visit during the hospital stay and schedule a follow-up appointment at their office shortly after discharge.

Week 44 — Regaining Birth Weight

By the second week, baby is feeding more frequently and in larger quantities, and should be close to regaining birth weight. You may notice baby beginning to track movement — following you across the room with their eyes. This early visual tracking is a healthy developmental milestone.

Mom remains significantly sleep-deprived. If breastfeeding, her breasts are large and nipples likely still tender. The uterus is actively shrinking, though it remains enlarged compared to its pre-pregnancy size. Nursing mothers tend to lose weight more quickly but also experience heightened hunger and thirst, as the body requires substantial energy to produce milk. Keep water easily accessible — a bottle within arm's reach at every feeding session makes a meaningful difference.

Communication between partners is critical during this phase. Both parents should openly discuss worries, stressors, and how to divide nighttime duties. If mom had vaginal tearing or a C-section, recovery is still in progress. Prepare ice packs, retrieve items when needed, and ensure she is not overexerting herself. All medications — including over-the-counter pain relievers, vitamins, and supplements — should be cleared by a physician, as many substances pass through breast milk.

Week 45 — Finding a Rhythm

Baby is now gaining five to seven ounces per week and beginning to focus on more complex shapes. Sleep averages 15 to 17 hours per day, broken into segments between feedings. The digestive system is working harder, and you may notice increased gas and spitting up. The umbilical cord stump will likely be dry enough to detach this week.

Mom's uterus continues shrinking, and weight loss may be noticeable. There is no clearance yet for exercise or sexual intercourse. She may experience bouts of urinary incontinence from muscles weakened during labor. If she had an episiotomy, stitches should dissolve by the end of this week. Her abdominal muscles remain stretched — some visible postpartum belly is entirely normal.

This is an ideal time to establish a family schedule, especially if a return to work is approaching. Consistent routines around feeding, napping, and diaper changes help both parents manage the cognitive load of early parenthood. Between feedings, short outdoor walks with baby — weather permitting — benefit everyone. A good rule: dress baby in one more layer than an older child would need.

Week 46 — Tummy Time & the Four-Week Checkup

The four-week pediatrician visit is a key milestone. Come prepared with questions: Is baby gaining enough weight? Are there skin changes to watch? What is the recommended vaccination schedule, and can it be adjusted? Is baby eating and sleeping enough? If your pediatrician doesn't feel like the right fit, finding a new one is straightforward and worth doing.

Introduce tummy time — placing baby on their stomach on a firm surface or on a parent's chest — to begin strengthening neck muscles. A baby gym with dangling toys encourages reaching and grasping. You may see baby mimic facial expressions, attempt to focus on your face, and possibly laugh for the first time.

Mom's hormones are gradually normalizing. The baby blues should be receding; if they are not, speak with her care provider. Her appetite is returning to normal, and light exercise like walking may be cleared — unless she had a C-section or her physician recommends waiting until the postpartum checkup. Kegel exercises can help rebuild pelvic-floor muscles weakened during delivery.

The Bottom Line

“The first month is about survival and support. Focus on establishing feeding, protecting sleep for both parents, and watching for postpartum mood changes in mothers and fathers alike. Everything else can wait.”
