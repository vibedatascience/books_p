The Second Month

The embryo transforms from a ball of cells into something recognizably human — complete with a beating heart, facial features, and tiny fingers — while morning sickness and fatigue announce the pregnancy from the inside out.

Week 5 — The Neural Tube

By Week 5, the embryo is the size of a small lemon seed and is beginning to look less like a cluster of cells and more like a tadpole. The three cell layers established last month are now building specific structures. The ectoderm is forming the neural tube — the foundation of the entire nervous system and spine. The mesoderm is developing the circulatory system, skeleton, and the very earliest structures of the heart and blood vessels. The endoderm connects to the placenta, which is now transporting nutrients to fuel growth.

The heart — about the size of a poppy seed — is beginning to take shape and starting to beat. The neural tube is open this week but will close by next week, eventually forming the brain and spinal cord. This is why early folic acid intake is so critical.

Outwardly, your partner looks much the same, but internally the changes are mounting: breast tenderness, frequent urination, occasional nausea, and the first signs of real fatigue. The first prenatal appointment should be scheduled by now if it hasn't been already. At home, taking on a larger share of household responsibilities helps offset the energy drain your partner is experiencing.

Week 6 — Your Baby Has a Face

The embryo is roughly the size of a small candy — perhaps a quarter inch from crown to rump — and facial features are starting to emerge. The eyes, ears, jaw, cheeks, and chin are beginning to form. Internally, the kidneys, liver, and lungs are taking shape. The heart is now beating at approximately 110 times per minute, pumping blood through tiny developing vessels. Small nubs that will become arms and legs are now visible.

Your partner may have a noticeable "glow" even though her waist hasn't changed. Her hair may be thicker and her nails stronger — both effects of pregnancy hormones. Nipples are darkening and breasts are enlarging. Mood swings driven by hormonal fluctuations are increasingly common.

Week 6 is the most common week for the first prenatal appointment. Expect urine and blood screening for nutrient deficiencies, along with weight and blood pressure checks. A pelvic exam may also be performed. Prepare by knowing the first and last day of your partner's most recent period, and bring a list of questions covering weight gain expectations, dietary guidelines, exercise limits, travel restrictions, and prenatal vitamin recommendations.

Be alert for signs of prenatal mood disorders. Anxiety and depression during pregnancy are common but often go unspoken. Checking in regularly and asking direct, compassionate questions is the most effective early intervention.

Week 7 — Hands and Feet

The baby is now the size of a blueberry — 10,000 times larger than at conception. Growth is staggering: the entire skeleton has formed, though it remains soft and pliable rather than hard bone. The arm nubs are dividing into hand, arm, and shoulder segments; the leg nubs into foot, knee, and leg segments. The digestive system, kidneys, liver, pancreas, and appendix are now developed. The brain is growing at a remarkable rate of approximately 100 cells per minute.

The uterus has doubled in size to accommodate the baby. The placenta has developed hairlike projections called villi that transfer nutrients from mother to baby. Morning sickness and food aversions may increase sharply this week. A heightened sense of smell — sometimes called "super smell" — is common and can trigger nausea from previously tolerable odors.

If the first prenatal appointment has already occurred, your doctor may have raised the topic of prenatal genetic or chromosomal testing. This is worth discussing as a couple before the next visit. At home, deep cleaning — steam-cleaning carpets, clearing out expired food, freshening trash receptacles — can meaningfully reduce nausea triggers. Soft cheeses should be avoided during pregnancy, but hard cheeses, crackers, and fresh produce make excellent light meals.

Week 8 — Fingers and Toes

The baby is now the size of a raspberry. Its heart is beating at an extraordinary 150 to 170 beats per minute — roughly twice the rate of an adult heart. Webbed fingers and toes are developing, along with eyelids. Primitive neural pathways to the brain and bronchial tubes extending toward the developing lungs are forming. The embryonic tail is nearly gone, and the baby is looking distinctly human.

Your partner's stomach remains relatively flat, but the expanded uterus may cause abdominal tenderness. Moodiness, heightened smell, morning sickness, food cravings, food aversions, frequent urination, and low energy are all in full effect. If the first prenatal appointment hasn't happened yet, it is now extremely urgent to schedule one.

Patience and active listening are the most valuable things a partner can offer right now. Hormonal shifts create genuine mood volatility — it is physiological, not personal. Finding low-key diversions and shared activities can help manage anxiety for both partners.

The Bottom Line

“Month 2 is when the pregnancy becomes physiologically real. The embryo transforms from a ball of cells into a recognizable form with a beating heart, a developing brain, facial features, and limb buds — all while remaining smaller than a raspberry.”

“For partners, this month demands two things above all: take over a meaningful share of household work (her fatigue is genuine and deepening), and show up emotionally with patience and presence as hormonal changes reshape her daily experience.”
