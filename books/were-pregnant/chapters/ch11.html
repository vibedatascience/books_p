The Eleventh Month

Weeks 47–50: Settling into routines, introducing the bottle, longer sleep stretches, and the two-month pediatric checkup.

Week 47 — Social Smiles

This may be the week you see your baby's first genuine smile — not a reflex, not gas, but a real response to social interaction. Baby is beginning to recognize faces and react to familiar voices. Sleep and feeding patterns are shifting: longer stretches at night, more extended periods of wakefulness during the day.

Growth is rapid. Baby is gaining approximately one inch per week in length and five to seven ounces per week in weight. Feeding occurs roughly every two hours, 8 to 10 times per day, with each session yielding two to five ounces. Baby also recognizes and responds to music.

Mom is approaching her pre-pregnancy physical condition. The uterus is nearly back to normal size, and if she has been doing Kegel exercises, incontinence should be tapering off. Nipple chapping is beginning to heal as her body adapts to nursing. For many mothers, this may be the final week of parental leave — making it an important time to maximize bonding and finalize childcare arrangements.

Week 48 — Introducing the Bottle

If the breastfeeding parent is returning to work, this is an ideal time to introduce baby to the bottle. This can mean pumped breast milk or formula — either is a valid choice. Some mothers pump or nurse for a year or more; others find it unsustainable. There is no single right approach. If baby resists the new nipple, try letting a small amount of milk touch baby's lips before attempting a full feeding.

Feeding and sleeping patterns are evening out. Baby continues eating up to 10 times per day, two to five ounces per session, and growing about an inch per week. This is an excellent opportunity for the non-nursing parent to participate in bottle feedings — a significant bonding experience.

If this is approximately six weeks postpartum, mom will likely have her postpartum checkup and may receive clearance to resume sexual activity and moderate exercise. Emotional well-being should be a specific topic at this appointment. Mom's uterus has returned to normal size. Weight loss continues, particularly for breastfeeding mothers, whose appetites may remain elevated. Come prepared with questions about how to support continued recovery.

Week 49 — Fewer Crying Episodes

A welcome development: unless baby has colic (which affects roughly one in five infants), crying episodes should be decreasing. Colic — defined as recurrent, prolonged, uncontrollable crying for three or more hours in an otherwise healthy infant — typically resolves by three to four months.

Baby should now be sleeping six to seven hours at a stretch at night, which means meaningfully more rest for both parents. Growth continues at about one inch and seven ounces per week. Feeding has evolved to four to five ounces every three to four hours. Sensory development is accelerating: baby is beginning to associate sights with sounds and tracking objects with increased precision.

Creating a consistent nighttime routine is key to preserving those longer sleep stretches. Feed baby well before bedtime — a practice sometimes called "tanking up" — to encourage a longer initial sleep period. White noise, ocean sounds, or soft music can reinforce the sleep cue. Mom's hormones continue to shift and she may produce less milk; her hair may also begin shedding, which is a normal postpartum phenomenon that will resolve on its own.

Week 50 — The Two-Month Checkup

Two months in, and routines are making daily life substantially more manageable. Baby is now feeding about six times within 24 hours, increasing intake with each passing week. Growth spurts are common at this stage — baby may suddenly demand more food right after a feeding. This is normal and temporary; it does not mean your supply is insufficient or your feeding approach is wrong.

The two-month pediatric appointment is a critical milestone. Most pediatricians will recommend beginning immunizations at this visit. Key questions to ask: What developmental milestones should we expect? Should baby be tracking us with their eyes? What is the recommended vaccination schedule, and what are the options for spacing vaccines? How much tummy time is appropriate?

Mom should be largely back to normal physically. If she is not exclusively breastfeeding, her period may return — which means she is ovulating and can become pregnant again. Discuss birth control with your partner and physician. Her body is adapting to postpartum hormonal changes, and her mood should feel more even. However, some mothers take longer to find their rhythm, and that is perfectly normal.

The Bottom Line

“Month two is when routines take hold and life begins to feel manageable again. Baby is sleeping longer, eating more efficiently, and developing genuine social responses. Use this period to establish patterns that will serve the family well in the months ahead.”
