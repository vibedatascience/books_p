The Third Month

The first trimester closes with a major milestone — the embryo officially becomes a fetus, miscarriage risk drops sharply, and first-trimester screening offers an early window into the baby's chromosomal health.

Week 9 — Starting to Look Like a Person

At roughly one inch long — about the size of an olive — the baby is beginning to look distinctly human. Joints are starting to move. Fingers and toes are now defined, and the mouth, nose, and eyes have become more distinct. The tiny heart has started beating, and for many couples this is the month when they first hear it, either at a prenatal appointment or through a handheld Doppler device at home.

All first-trimester physical effects are now in full swing: mood swings, fatigue, nausea, and frequent urination. Stress is a significant concern at this stage. A home Doppler — available for rental from companies like BabyBeat at roughly $25–$35 per month — can provide reassurance between appointments by allowing you to hear the heartbeat at home. It may take practice to locate the signal, but it becomes easier as the pregnancy progresses.

Ginger is a well-documented natural remedy for pregnancy-related nausea. Fresh ginger root, peeled and shredded, can be boiled into a simple tea with honey — an easy and effective way to ease morning sickness.

Week 10 — Finally a Fetus

Week 10 marks a landmark transition: the embryo officially becomes a fetus. All vital organs are now in place. Bones and cartilage are forming, along with knees and ankles. Elbows are beginning to work. Tooth buds are developing below the gum line. If the baby is a boy, testes are starting to form. The baby now has a bulging forehead housing a rapidly growing brain, and fingernails and toenails are emerging.

Your partner's uterus has doubled in size — from roughly pear-sized to grapefruit-sized. This may be the week the baby bump first becomes visible. Ligament pain in the abdomen is increasingly common as tissues stretch to accommodate the growing uterus. Prenatal yoga can help manage this discomfort. Maternity clothes may soon become necessary — jeans extenders and belly bands (around $25) can extend the life of regular clothing during this transition.

The second prenatal appointment typically occurs around this time. Expect a review of initial prenatal test results, along with checks of weight, blood pressure, and urine. This is also when genetic blood testing may be offered — particularly for women over 35 or those with risk factors — and when the nuchal translucency (NT) scan for Down syndrome and other chromosomal abnormalities is typically scheduled.

Week 11 — Teeth and Bones

The baby is roughly 1.5 inches long, weighs about a quarter ounce, and its head makes up about half of its total body size. Bones are hardening, the torso is lengthening, and hair follicles along with fingernail and toenail beds are developing. Tooth buds continue forming beneath the gums, and organs are growing steadily.

Your partner has likely gained one to five pounds, and her baby bump may be on display. Many couples follow the "12-week rule" — waiting until the end of the first trimester to announce the pregnancy publicly, because miscarriage risk drops significantly as the first trimester closes.

However, consider telling your closest support network earlier than 12 weeks. If a miscarriage were to occur, these are the people you would call for help and support. Learning about a pregnancy and a loss simultaneously — as some couples experience when they wait — can be an especially painful situation for everyone involved. Tell the people you would need in a crisis, and save the broader announcement for after the first trimester.

Week 12 — Reflexes

The baby has doubled in size over the past few weeks and is now about the size of a lime. Intestines, eyes, and ears are in place. The baby has begun developing reflexes — making sucking motions to prepare for feeding — and is starting to respond to external stimuli, though it remains too small for your partner to feel any movement.

The top of the uterus is now above the pelvic bones. Your partner may experience discomfort lying on her stomach as the uterus presses against the abdomen. Occasional indigestion is common. On a positive note, the severe moodiness and crushing fatigue of early pregnancy may begin to ease as the first trimester draws to a close.

This is an excellent time to plan a trip together. The second trimester is generally the most comfortable period for travel — energy returns, nausea fades, and the belly isn't yet large enough to restrict mobility. Avoid scheduling any flights after Week 36.

Week 13 — Call It a Trimester

The first trimester is officially complete. The baby's head, previously half its body size, is now about one-third — the head and body are becoming more proportionate. The baby now has fingerprints. If it is a girl, her ovaries already contain eggs. The level of structural development at this stage is remarkable.

Your partner most likely has a visible baby bump. Her breasts have begun producing colostrum — the nutrient-rich pre-milk that will be the baby's first food. The risk of miscarriage has decreased significantly. Her sex drive may increase, driven by the same hormonal changes that have been causing mood swings.

Headaches — including migraines — are a common complaint as the first trimester ends, driven by pregnancy hormones. Most standard headache remedies are off-limits during pregnancy. Prevention is the best approach: regular meals, stress reduction, adequate rest, and quiet time to decompress. Constipation is another frequent issue as the expanding uterus compresses the digestive tract. Smaller, more frequent meals (five or six per day instead of three) and consistent fluid intake — water and juice — can help significantly.

The Bottom Line

“Month 3 closes the first trimester with tangible milestones: the embryo becomes a fetus with all organs in place, the baby develops reflexes and fingerprints, and miscarriage risk drops to its lowest point yet. For many couples, this is when the pregnancy finally feels real — and when the world gets to find out.”

“Don't skip the nuchal translucency scan window (Weeks 10–13). Discuss your announcement timing thoughtfully — tell your inner circle early, save the public reveal for after the first trimester. And start planning that second-trimester trip while your partner still has the energy and mobility to enjoy it.”
