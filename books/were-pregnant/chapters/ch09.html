The Ninth Month

The final stretch: baby is fully developed and gaining last-minute weight, while parents shift into high alert for delivery — which statistically may not arrive on the due date.

Week 36 — In the Homestretch

The baby weighs approximately 6 pounds and measures 18–19 inches in length — about the size of a head of romaine lettuce. The circulatory, musculoskeletal, and digestive systems are all fully operational. The baby is fully developed and now primarily gaining weight. Movements in the womb are more subtle as space becomes increasingly limited. Cheeks are filling out, and the baby is forming meconium (its first stool) from swallowed amniotic fluid.

Hormones are loosening the mother’s connective tissue in preparation for delivery. “Lightening” — the baby dropping lower into the pelvis — may still be in progress. Swelling in the lower extremities, back pain, and poor sleep are common and can cause significant irritability. Appointments are now weekly. The doctor will check the baby’s position, uterine height, and continue monitoring urine, weight, and blood pressure. An ultrasound may be ordered to confirm fetal positioning.

Note on air travel: most experts consider flying safe up to 36 weeks, but after this point, most doctors and airlines advise against it. If air travel is absolutely necessary, a doctor’s note will be required to board. Encourage rest at every opportunity — even at the expense of social obligations. Those commitments can wait.

Week 37 — Early Term

A baby born at week 37 is considered “at term” and is no longer classified as premature. Only about 5 percent of babies arrive on their actual due date, and roughly 6.4 percent are born at 37 weeks (more common with multiples). The average fetus weighs around 6.5 pounds, though there is considerable variation. Fat continues to accumulate, and the baby is still inhaling and exhaling amniotic fluid while shifting from side to side in the womb.

The mother may experience a surge of energy as birth approaches — a phenomenon commonly associated with “nesting.” Pelvic pain and the urge to urinate are more pronounced as the baby drops. Stretch marks may become visible. This burst of energy often manifests as an instinct to organize, clean, and prepare the home environment for the baby’s arrival.

Review the action plan this week. Everyone on the emergency contact list should be on high alert. Stay in daily contact with anyone included in the birth logistics — family members, friends who are watching pets, or colleagues covering at work. Keep your phone charged and accessible at all times. Encourage the mother to eat small, frequent meals or snacks to maintain strength and continue supporting the baby’s final weight gain.

Week 38 — Ready to Meet You

The baby now weighs approximately 7 pounds and measures close to 20 inches. It is shedding the vernix — the greasy white coating that has protected the skin throughout development. Toenails have reached the tips of the toes, and the vocal cords are fully developed. Everything is ready; the baby is simply gaining final weight and waiting for nature to determine the timing.

The cervix may be softening and beginning to dilate. The mother’s appetite is often poor — the baby is taking up so much space that large meals are physically uncomfortable. Protein shakes or bars are a practical alternative. Anxiety about delivery is entirely normal and almost universal at this stage.

Unless a C-section is scheduled, the timing of maternity leave is a judgment call. Some couples prefer to log an extra week of work and pay, but this must be weighed against the risk of going into labor at the office. If the baby is breech (positioned upright rather than head-down), the doctor will have already scheduled or is in the process of scheduling a C-section. A cesarean delivery involves incisions through the abdomen and uterus, requires a longer hospital stay, and results in a more involved recovery process.

Week 39 — Any Day Now

The baby weighs between 7 and 8 pounds, measures 19–21 inches, and has essentially stopped growing until delivery. Brain development continues — and will for the first several years of life. Old skin is sloughing off and being replaced by new skin. The baby’s pigmentation is transitioning from pink to a whitish-gray that will change again upon exposure to air after birth.

The mother may be deeply frustrated, wishing labor would begin. The anxiety of the unknown compounds the physical discomfort and irritability. Lightning crotch — that sharp, sudden pelvic pain caused by the baby pinching nerves — may continue. All previous discomforts persist: poor sleep, difficulty eating, and slow-going daily activities.

Encourage self-care: warm baths, plenty of rest, small meals, and feet up as much as possible. This is a good time to finalize any remaining items on the to-do list — nursery details, a final clean of the house, and a last check of the hospital bag. Weekly check-in appointments continue.

Week 40 — The Due Date

The official due date has arrived. The baby weighs between 6 and 9 pounds and measures 19–22 inches, though healthy babies fall anywhere along that spectrum. About 30 percent of all babies are born during this week — but that means the majority are not. Your provider will generally not allow the pregnancy to extend past 42 weeks.

The cervix is softening and dilating. The mother is likely experiencing every discomfort simultaneously — physical, emotional, and psychological. She needs support in every dimension: practical help around the house, emotional reassurance, and a partner who is fully present and prepared to be the point person for all logistics once labor begins. Have parking information, floor numbers, and recovery room details ready to share with family and visitors.

Weeks 41–42 — Past the Due Date

Around 16 percent of babies are born in week 41 and another 1 percent during week 42. If the baby hasn’t arrived, the doctor will discuss inducing labor or proceeding with a C-section. The baby is not considered post-term until 42 weeks, and most providers will intervene before that point. The baby is full-term and continues adding fat; brain development is ongoing.

Missing the due date can be emotionally difficult for the mother, creating a sense of letdown on top of already-running anxiety. The best thing a partner can do is remain calm, available, and attentive. Keep the hospital bag by the front door (or bring it with you to work and back each day). Practice breathing techniques from any Lamaze or childbirth classes you attended — even 15 minutes each evening helps keep the methods fresh. And don’t forget self-care for the partner: a quick run, a few minutes of meditation, or a walk can help maintain your own equilibrium during this waiting period.

The Bottom Line

“Month nine is a waiting game with high stakes and unpredictable timing. The baby is fully developed and ready — the only remaining question is when. Keep the hospital bag packed, the action plan reviewed, and the support network on standby. Most babies do not arrive on their due date, and going past it is entirely normal. Stay calm, stay prepared, and trust the process.”
