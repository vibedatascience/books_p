I'm So Over Being Pregnant

When will this baby actually arrive — and can cervical checks tell you anything useful?

When Babies Actually Arrive

Without intervention, the average first-time mother goes into labor 8 days after her due date. Close to 30% of babies are born in the 39th week, making it the single most common week. About 70% of babies arrive before the due date — but this includes all births, and first babies tend to come later.

More useful is the conditional question: if you're still pregnant at the start of a given week, what's the chance you'll deliver that week? At 38 weeks, there's a 25% chance. At 39 weeks, 46%. If you reach your due date with no baby, there's a 60% chance you'll deliver in the next 7 days. At 42 weeks, virtually all doctors will induce.

Cervical Checks

Doctors begin cervical checks late in the third trimester to assess progress toward labor. They measure dilation (how open the cervix is, from 0 to 10 cm) and effacement (how thin/short the cervix has become, reported as a percentage). Though doctors tend to emphasize dilation, effacement is actually the better predictor of when labor will start.

A UK study measured effacement by ultrasound at 37 weeks, then tracked when women went into labor. For women more than 60% effaced at 37 weeks, nearly all (about 98%) went into labor before their due date. For women less than 40% effaced, almost none (under 10%) delivered before the due date.

There's also a composite measure called the Bishop score (0–13), which combines cervical position, effacement, dilation, and the baby's station. A score of 6 or above indicates you're fairly advanced. The Bishop score is also highly predictive of whether an induction will result in vaginal delivery vs. C-section.

  • 3
    Ask about effacement, not just dilation. Cervical length is more predictive of labor onset than how many centimeters you're dilated.
