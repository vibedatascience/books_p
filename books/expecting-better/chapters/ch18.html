The Labor Numbers

How long does labor actually take, what are the common problems, and when is a C-section the right call?

How Long It Takes

Labor has three stages. The first — dilation from 0 to 10 centimeters — is by far the longest, and is itself divided into early labor (0–3 cm, mild contractions, can last days), active labor (3–7 cm, more intense), and transition (7–10 cm, the hardest part). The second stage is pushing, and the third is delivery of the placenta.

The textbook standard — cervical dilation of at least 1 cm per hour — comes from a 1955 study of 500 women. A more recent 2002 study of 1,300 women in Hawaii found the real average is 1–2 cm per hour, with significant acceleration later: the average woman takes nearly 6 hours to go from 3 to 7 cm, but only 90 minutes from 7 to 10 cm. Before 7 cm, it's common to go 2–3 hours with no apparent progress. This is normal, not a sign of failure.

Common Labor Problems

Three main things can go wrong during labor. First, dilation can stall or proceed too slowly, which may require Pitocin or lead to a C-section if the baby shows signs of distress. Second, the baby can get stuck during pushing, especially if the baby is large or the pelvis is small. Doctors may use forceps or a vacuum extractor — both safe for the baby, though they increase vaginal tearing for Mom. Third, the baby may be facing up (“sunny side up”), making pushing harder. The baby's position often changes during labor, so prebirth ultrasounds can't predict this.

Breech & C-Sections

About 25% of babies are breech at 28 weeks, but most rotate on their own. By delivery, only 3–4% remain breech. If your baby is still breech at 37 weeks, doctors can attempt an ECV (external cephalic version) — manually turning the baby from outside. It's successful about half the time. If it fails, you'll almost certainly have a scheduled C-section: randomized studies show vaginal breech delivery is slightly riskier.

VBAC (vaginal birth after Cesarean) is possible but carries some increased risk. An Australian study found that women planning a VBAC had more infant complications and maternal hemorrhage — about 2.5% vs. 0.8% in the planned repeat C-section group. About half of attempted VBACs end in a C-section anyway. If you do attempt one, choose a doctor experienced with them.

The Bottom Line

“Labor times vary a lot. Average dilation time is 1 to 2 centimeters an hour after active labor starts.”

“Emergency C-sections are a good option to have, but a C-section should not be your first choice … unless your baby is breech or (probably) if you've had a C-section before.”
