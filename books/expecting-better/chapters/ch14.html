Premature Birth

What are the survival odds week by week — and why bed rest probably doesn't help.

Survival by Week

Preterm birth — delivery before 37 weeks — occurs in about 12% of US pregnancies. The outcomes depend enormously on how early the baby arrives. Survival outside the womb is possible as early as 22 weeks, though unlikely. By 24 weeks, more than half of babies survive their first year. By 28 weeks — just 6½ months into pregnancy — the survival rate is almost 95%. By 34 weeks, it's 99%.

Until 34 weeks, the chance of having a baby in any given week is less than 1 in 100. Before 30 weeks it's less than 1 in 500. Still, prematurity has consequences: a study of 5-year-olds born before 30 weeks found that 75% had at least one disability (vs. 27% among full-term children) and their IQs were 5–14 points lower on average.

Bed Rest

Bed rest is prescribed to as many as 20% of pregnant women, yet there is no compelling evidence that it prevents preterm labor. In a study of 1,200 women with threatened preterm labor, 400 were put on bed rest and 800 were not. Preterm birth rates were nearly identical: 7.9% in the bed rest group vs. 8.5% in the control group.

Reviews of the literature are damning. One 2011 paper concluded: “There have been no complications of pregnancy for which the literature consistently demonstrates a benefit to antepartum bed rest.” Another was titled simply: “Lack of Evidence for Prescription of Antepartum Bed Rest.”

Worse, bed rest has documented negative consequences: bone loss, muscle atrophy, weight loss, decreased infant birth weight, increased blood clot risk, and financial strain on families. A 2009 survey found that more than half of OBs acknowledged bed rest has no or minimal benefit — yet 90% of them still prescribed it.

The Bottom Line

“Survival outside the womb is possible (although not likely) as early as 22 weeks. Survival dramatically increases with continued gestation after this point. By 28 weeks, more than 90 percent of babies survive, and by 34 weeks it's 99 percent.”

“Delaying birth after the onset of labor is difficult, but usually can be done for a few days. Delaying even just a day or two can have large impacts on survival.”

“There is no evidence that bed rest will prevent preterm labor. Avoid it.”
