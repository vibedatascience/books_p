The Vices: Caffeine, Alcohol & Tobacco

What the evidence actually says about coffee, wine, and cigarettes during pregnancy.

Alcohol

The universal recommendation in the United States is no alcohol during pregnancy. But similar organizations in other countries indicate that occasional drinking is fine. If light drinking were truly harmful, we'd expect to see higher rates of fetal alcohol syndrome in Europe, where pregnant women commonly have a glass of wine with dinner. We don't — if anything, rates are higher in the US.

The biology explains why there's a difference between light and heavy drinking. When you drink, your liver processes alcohol into acetaldehyde (which is toxic) and then into harmless acetate. If you drink slowly, your liver keeps up and little toxin reaches the fetus. If you drink fast, your liver can't keep up and neither can the baby's. Speed matters as much as quantity.

The best studies of light drinking come from Australia and England, where drinking during pregnancy is less stigmatized — meaning the women who drink aren't systematically different from those who don't. An Australian study of ~3,000 women tracked children from birth to age 14. Women drinking 2–6 drinks per week actually had children with fewer behavior problems than nondrinkers. A separate Australian study of ~7,200 women tested children's IQ at age 14 — no difference between light drinkers and abstainers. A large English study found the same: no impact of light drinking on child IQ at age 8.

A large Danish study of ~100,000 women showed no impact of up to 8 drinks a week on child IQ at age 5. For premature birth, Italian and Danish studies found no link with light drinking — if anything, light drinkers had slightly lower rates.

The one area with mixed evidence is first-trimester miscarriage. A 2012 Danish study of ~100,000 women found that 4+ drinks per week was associated with double the miscarriage risk. This argues for more caution in the first trimester specifically.

  • 1
    Light drinking shows no negative effects on baby IQ or behavior across multiple large studies in Australia, England, and Denmark.
  • 2
    Binge drinking (5+ drinks at a time) is clearly harmful — linked to language delays, cognitive deficits, and physical deformities. No ambiguity here.
  • 3
    The author's guideline: up to 1 drink a day in the second and third trimesters, 1–2 drinks a week in the first trimester. Speed matters — drink like a European adult, not a fraternity brother.
Caffeine

Recommendations on caffeine range from none (Mayo Clinic) to 200mg/day (ACOG) to 300mg (some OBs). An 8oz Starbucks coffee has ~165mg; McDonald's brewed has ~100mg. The main concern is miscarriage.

Studying caffeine in pregnancy has a unique problem: nausea. Nausea is a sign of a healthy pregnancy — nauseous women are less likely to miscarry. But nauseous women also avoid coffee. So when studies find that coffee drinkers miscarry more, it may not be the coffee causing it — it may be that healthier pregnancies make women too nauseous to drink coffee. This confound is nearly impossible to fully separate.

A 2010 Maryland study with daily diet diaries found no relationship between caffeine and miscarriage. A 2008 study of ~2,400 women found no significant link up to 2 cups/day, and women drinking 3.5+ cups actually had lower miscarriage rates than nondrinkers. A Danish study of ~100,000 women found no impact up to 3 cups/day. One California study did find higher miscarriage for 2+ cups, but notably found no effect among women who reduced their intake — suggesting the nausea confound was driving the results.

For later pregnancy, the evidence is stronger because a randomized controlled trial exists. Danish researchers gave 1,207 coffee-drinking pregnant women either caffeinated or decaffeinated instant coffee (women didn't know which). The caffeinated group consumed ~200mg more caffeine per day. Their babies were identical: same birth weight (7 lbs 12 oz), same length, same gestational age, same head circumference.

Tobacco

Unlike alcohol and caffeine, the evidence on smoking is unambiguous: smoking during pregnancy is dangerous. A study of 1 million+ births in Missouri (1989–2005) found that smokers were more than twice as likely to have a baby small for gestational age (17% vs 7.5%), had higher rates of placental abruption (1.27% vs 0.71%), and more preterm births (13.6% vs 10.6%). Women smoking just 1–9 cigarettes per day had the same elevated risks as heavier smokers.

A Dutch study found that smoking more than 9 cigarettes per day after 25 weeks produced babies about 7 ounces lighter — a 6% reduction in body weight. A UK study found children of mothers who smoked 1–9 cigarettes/day were 4 times more likely to die of SIDS; for 20+ cigarettes, the risk was 9 times higher. 86% of SIDS deaths in England were among children of mothers who smoked.

The good news: quitting anytime during pregnancy helps. Randomized trials of smoking cessation programs show that women who quit had babies ~14 ounces heavier and a 28% lower chance of preterm birth. There's no evidence that smoking before pregnancy causes problems — the benefits of quitting start immediately.

  • 6
    Do not smoke. Even moderate smoking (1–9 cigarettes/day) doubles the risk of a small baby and quadruples the SIDS risk. There is no safe level.
  • 7
    Quitting at any point helps. Later-pregnancy smoking has the biggest effect on birth weight, so even quitting mid-pregnancy makes a significant difference.
The Bottom Line

“There is no good evidence that light drinking during pregnancy negatively impacts your baby.”

“In moderation, coffee is fine. All evidence supports having up to 2 cups. Much of the evidence supports 3 to 4 cups.”

“Smoking during pregnancy is dangerous for your baby.”
