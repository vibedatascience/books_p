The Epidural

What the randomized trials actually show about epidural benefits, risks, and impact on the baby.

How It Works

An epidural numbs your lower body by delivering anesthesia through a catheter threaded into the membrane surrounding the spine. It's administered during the first stage of labor (dilation) and sometimes turned off for pushing. It was used in about two-thirds of US births in 2008; at some hospitals, the rate exceeds 90%. The modern epidural has been in use since the 1960s, replacing older methods like chloroform and “twilight sleep” (morphine + scopolamine).

Impact on the Baby

From the baby's standpoint, the epidural mostly doesn't matter. Randomized trials show no difference in APGAR scores, NICU admissions, or fetal distress. There's also no evidence of an impact on breast-feeding. The one indirect concern: epidurals make mothers more likely to run a fever (a known side effect, not an infection), which leads doctors to treat the baby with unnecessary antibiotics. In one study, 90% of babies born to mothers with epidural fevers received antibiotics — and none of them actually needed the drugs.

Impact on Mom

The epidural is excellent pain relief — randomized trials confirm it, though it probably doesn't need confirmation. But it comes with trade-offs.

Instruments: Women with epidurals are more likely to need forceps or a vacuum extractor during delivery. Pushing time: About 15 minutes longer on average. Baby position: Slightly higher chance of the baby facing up (“sunny side”), likely because the epidural reduces movement during labor, which normally helps the baby rotate. Pitocin: More likely to be needed, since the epidural slows contractions. Other: Higher chance of low blood pressure, needing a catheter, and inability to walk until the epidural wears off.

C-section rates are mixed. Overall rates are similar (10.7% with epidural vs. 9.7% without), but emergency C-sections for fetal distress are more common with an epidural (3.5% vs. 2.4%). The epidural does not lengthen the dilation phase or cause long-term backache, despite common fears.

One specific risk: a wet tap (needle accidentally entering spinal fluid) occurs in about 1 in 200 procedures, even at good hospitals. It leads to a severe multi-day headache 40% of the time. Make sure your anesthesiologist is experienced.

Natural Pain Relief

If you go without an epidural, natural pain relief options include Lamaze, the Bradley Method, and Hypnobabies. Evidence on these is thin because the women who invest in learning them are already highly committed to natural childbirth. Aromatherapy has no impact. Acupuncture has some limited evidence of reducing pain and drug use, but studies are small and mixed. Breathing techniques won't hurt, but we can't confirm from data that they help.

The Bottom Line

“But it increases the chance of some complications for the mother.”
