Beyond Pain Relief

The evidence-based birth plan: water during labor, doulas, fetal monitoring, episiotomy, and Pitocin after birth.

Water Breaking First

Contrary to television, only about 10% of women have their water break before contractions start. Most go into labor within 12 hours. Standard practice is to induce if labor doesn't begin soon after. A large randomized trial compared inducing within 12 hours vs. letting women wait up to 4 days. There was no difference in C-section rates or baby outcomes, but maternal infection rates were significantly higher in the waiting group — especially when doctors performed vaginal exams. If your water breaks first, heading to the hospital within ~12 hours is a reasonable plan.

Eating & Drinking During Labor

Most hospitals restrict food during labor, a policy based on a 1946 paper about aspiration risk under general anesthesia. Today, C-sections are performed under local anesthesia 90% of the time, and the estimated risk of maternal death from aspiration is 2 in 10 million births. A 2009 review of 12,000 women who ate and drank freely during labor found zero aspiration-related problems. Sports drinks and clear juices are a good compromise — they maintain energy without the (theoretical) solid-food risk.

Get a Doula

A doula is a trained labor companion who provides physical and emotional support. Randomized trials show large effects. In one study, women randomly assigned a doula were half as likely to have a C-section (13% vs. 25%) and less likely to use an epidural (64% vs. 76%). A 1991 study found similar results: less than half the epidural rate, shorter labor, half the C-section rate, and half the forceps rate.

Notably, doula benefits are not limited to women avoiding epidurals. C-section rates were lower even among women who used an epidural in the doula group.

Continuous Fetal Monitoring

About 85% of US laboring women are hooked up to a continuous fetal monitor. The idea is to detect distress early. But a 2006 review found that continuous monitoring was 1.6x more likely to lead to a C-section and 2.4x more likely to lead to a C-section specifically for heart-rate concerns — with no improvement in baby outcomes. No differences in APGAR scores, NICU time, or fetal death. Doctors appear to overreact to normal heart-rate dips.

Intermittent monitoring (checking every 20–60 minutes with a Doppler) is equally safe and keeps you mobile. ACOG doesn't recommend continuous monitoring for low-risk pregnancies, yet most hospitals insist on it.

Episiotomy: Don't

An episiotomy — cutting the perineum to widen the opening — was used in 60% of US births in 1979. Randomized trials comparing routine episiotomy (72% of women) to selective use (27%) found the routine group had more perineal injury, more stitches, more blood loss, more pain at discharge, and more healing complications. Severe tearing was equally common in both groups. The procedure has dropped to ~25% by 2004, but it should be even lower.

Pitocin After Birth

Postpartum hemorrhage is one of the most common delivery complications. Randomized trials show that giving Pitocin (synthetic oxytocin) right after birth dramatically reduces the risk of significant blood loss. Side effects include higher blood pressure, more post-birth pain, and some vomiting — but these are minor compared to the hemorrhage prevention. This one is worth accepting.
