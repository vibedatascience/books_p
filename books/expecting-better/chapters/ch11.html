Pink and Blue

When and how you can learn your baby's sex — and whether old wives' tales have any basis.

How to Find Out

There are several ways to learn your baby's sex before birth. If you do CVS (first trimester) or amniocentesis (second trimester), the sex is part of the genetic mapping — boys have XY chromosomes, girls have XX. If you skip invasive testing, the 20-week ultrasound is the standard reveal, though sex can sometimes be seen as early as 12 weeks (especially for boys) and usually by 15–16 weeks.

A newer option uses cell-free fetal DNA in Mom's blood — the same technology behind NIPT screening. Because a small amount of baby's DNA circulates in your bloodstream, labs can look for a Y chromosome. In a study of 201 women, the test correctly identified 71 of 77 girls and 105 of 112 boys. This test is primarily used for clinical purposes (e.g., families with Y-linked genetic diseases) but is becoming more commercially available.

Old Wives' Tales

The most popular folk method is fetal heart rate — the claim that girls have faster heartbeats. A study of 500 pregnancies tested this directly. The average female heart rate was 151.7 bpm and the average male was 154.9 bpm — not significantly different, and if anything, boys were slightly faster. The paper concluded there are "no significant differences between male and female fetal heart rate during the first trimester."

Other common predictions — belly position (high vs. low), belly shape, food cravings — have no support in the medical literature. They all work about 50% of the time, which is the same as random guessing.

Can You Choose?

If you're willing to use assisted reproductive technology, yes. Sperm sorting can select for X or Y chromosome sperm for artificial insemination, with a high (though not perfect) success rate. IVF combined with preimplantation genetic diagnosis can select embryos by sex.

For natural conception, the popular Shettles Method claims that having sex several days before ovulation favors girls (slower but longer-lived X sperm) while sex at ovulation favors boys (faster Y sperm). There is no evidence this works. A 1995 study in the New England Journal of Medicine tracked a cohort of women and found no relationship between timing of sex relative to ovulation and offspring sex.

The Bottom Line

“If you want to learn your baby's sex before birth, you can do so through CVS, amniocentesis, or ultrasound.”

“There's no affirmative evidence that fetal heart rate or other old wives' tales do a good job of predicting gender.”

“You cannot increase your chances of a particular gender by changing the timing of sex before conception.”
