Drug Safety

Which medications are safe during pregnancy, and how to interpret the FDA's classification system.

How Drugs Reach the Baby

Your baby develops in the uterus, connected to you through the placenta — an organ that transfers nutrients from mother to baby and waste products back out. Not long ago, doctors believed the placenta was an impenetrable barrier. We now know that's wrong: virtually every drug you take — over-the-counter, prescription, or illegal — reaches the baby through a process called passive diffusion.

There are two exceptions. Some drug molecules are simply too large to pass through the placenta — heparin (a blood thinner) is a classic example. Others get trapped in the placenta itself, which acts as a depot and never passes them along. But for the vast majority of medications — painkillers, antibiotics, antidepressants — the baby is exposed to at least some of what you take.

The FDA Classification System

The FDA categorizes drugs into five classes: A, B, C, D, and X, from safest to most dangerous. Categories A and B are safe. D and X should be avoided. The real problem is Category C — which accounts for roughly 70% of all drugs — where evidence is ambiguous.

Category A means well-controlled human studies have shown no risk. Almost nothing qualifies. The one clear example is folic acid, which not only is safe but actively prevents birth defects: women who take it are about 70% less likely to have a baby with a neural tube defect like spina bifida. A large Norwegian study also linked folic acid supplementation before conception to lower rates of autism.

Category B means either human studies show no increased risk or animal studies show no fetal harm. Tylenol (acetaminophen) is the most common example. A Danish study of over 100,000 women — half of whom used acetaminophen during pregnancy — found no increase in birth defects, miscarriage, stillbirth, or low birth weight. In most other countries, acetaminophen is effectively Category A. The US system is more stringent.

Category C is the problematic catch-all: no adequate human studies, and either animal studies showed risk or no animal studies exist. About 70% of drugs fall here, meaning the FDA's position on most medications is essentially "we don't know." A drug with reassuring small human studies and another with animal evidence of harm can both be Category C. If your doctor prescribes a Category C drug, push for details about the actual evidence, not just the letter grade.

Category D drugs have demonstrated evidence of fetal risk but might be justified in serious situations. An example is tetracycline, an antibiotic linked to tooth discoloration based on a small 1964 study of just 9 children. Category X drugs should never be taken. Accutane is the textbook example: among 59 pregnancies with accidental exposure, 20% ended in miscarriage and 35% had major birth defects.
