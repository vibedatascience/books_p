Miscarriage Fears

What are the actual numbers — and how do they change week by week?

Risk by Week

Everyone knows the first trimester carries the highest risk of miscarriage, but concrete numbers are hard to come by. The convention of waiting until 12 weeks to announce a pregnancy reinforces the idea that something magic happens at that threshold. In reality, miscarriage risk declines gradually — there's no sharp drop at 12 weeks.

Before ~5 weeks (one week past your missed period), a pregnancy is considered "chemical" rather than "clinical" — detectable by a test but not yet visible on ultrasound. Many pregnancies are lost in this window, perhaps as many as half. These are the losses discussed in Chapter 3.

After 6 weeks, when doctors can see the pregnancy on ultrasound, researchers can track outcomes by week. Studies following women whose first normal prenatal visits came at different points show a clear pattern: if everything looks normal at 6 weeks, the overall chance of miscarriage is about 11%. By 8 weeks with a normal visit, it's ~6%. By 11 weeks, less than 2%.

What Affects Your Risk

Age is the biggest factor. One study found miscarriage rates of 4.4% for women under 20, 6.7% for ages 20–35, and nearly 19% for women over 35. This likely reflects a higher rate of chromosomal problems in older eggs. IVF pregnancies also carry higher risk — one large study reported 30% miscarriage for IVF versus 19% for natural conception.

Previous miscarriage matters too. An English study showed ~4–5% miscarriage for first pregnancies or women with a previous successful pregnancy, but ~25% for women with a prior miscarriage. This sounds alarming, but the vast majority of women who miscarry go on to have successful pregnancies.

During early pregnancy, vaginal bleeding is a warning sign — 13% of women with bleeding miscarried versus 4.2% without. Bleeding is very common in the first trimester and usually not a problem, but it does indicate slightly higher risk. Lack of nausea is another mild signal: nauseous women are less likely to miscarry.

After 12 weeks, miscarriage becomes much less common. Most studies put the risk at 1–2%. One study of ~300,000 women found rates as low as 0.6% after 15 weeks.

The Bottom Line

“Around 10 to 15 percent of pregnancies that are developing normally at 6 weeks will end in miscarriage. This rate declines quickly over the first trimester, and falls to around 1 to 2 percent by 11 or 12 weeks.”

“Older age and previous miscarriage increase your risk.”
