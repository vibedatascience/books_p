The Aftermath

Decisions in the delivery room right after birth: cord clamping, vitamin K, eye antibiotics, and cord-blood banking.

Delayed Cord Clamping

Rather than cutting the umbilical cord immediately, delayed cord clamping waits a couple of minutes so the baby can reabsorb blood from the placenta. For premature infants, this is clearly beneficial: it roughly halves the need for blood transfusions for both anemia and low blood pressure.

For full-term babies, the evidence is more mixed. On the plus side, delayed clamping leads to higher iron levels that persist for at least 6 months. On the negative side, some studies show a 40% increase in risk of jaundice that needs treatment. Both conditions are very treatable in the US. The trade-off is straightforward: lower anemia risk vs. higher jaundice risk. Since anemia is uncommon in the US (good nutrition), the benefits are smaller here than in the developing world.

Vitamin K Shots

Vitamin K supplementation has been standard since the 1960s, given as a shot within the first hours of life. It prevents bleeding disorders that affect up to 1.5% of babies in the first week and a rarer but much more dangerous late-onset bleeding (1 in 10,000) between 2–12 weeks that often causes severe brain damage or death.

In the early 1990s, two small UK studies suggested a possible link to childhood cancer. But subsequent research found no support: there was no increase in childhood cancer rates after the shots became standard in the 1960s, and attempts to replicate the findings failed. The American Academy of Pediatrics reaffirmed in 2003 that the benefits in preventing bleeding are large and the cancer concern is unsubstantiated.

Eye Antibiotics

Antibiotic drops are applied to newborns' eyes to prevent blindness from gonorrhea or chlamydia exposure during birth. This prevents 80–90% of infections. Since pregnant women are routinely tested for STIs, untreated infections are increasingly rare, and many European countries have dropped this practice with no increase in blindness. Still, it's legally mandated in most US states, has no real downside beyond mild redness, and you probably won't be given a choice.

Cord-Blood Banking

Private cord-blood banks will aggressively market to you starting mid-pregnancy. The idea is that stored stem cells might be useful for treating diseases. Currently, the main use is as an alternative to bone marrow transplant for leukemia — and the child's own cord blood typically can't be used for the child; it would be for a sibling.

For families without a known blood disorder, the chance of ever using banked cord blood is about 1 in 20,000. The companies argue future technology will expand the uses, but technology for creating stem cells from regular cells is also advancing. At a few thousand dollars for storage, the math isn't compelling for most families. Public cord-blood donation, which is free and helps others, is the more impactful option.

The Bottom Line

“Delayed cord clamping: a good idea if the baby is born before 37 weeks. If the baby is full term, it's up to you to trade off the (possibly) higher risk of jaundice with the lower risk of anemia.”

“Vitamin K shots: effective at preventing bleeding, and the claims that they increase the risk of cancer are unsubstantiated.”

“Cord-blood banking: very unlikely to be useful for your family given current technology. Public cord-blood banking is worth considering.”
