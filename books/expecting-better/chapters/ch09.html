The Surprising Perils of Gardening

Cat litter, hair dye, hot tubs, flying, and the lifestyle restrictions that are (mostly) overblown.

Cat Litter & Gardening

The worry about cat litter is toxoplasmosis — the same parasite discussed with food restrictions. But despite the emphasis on cats, a European study of pregnant women found no evidence that cats matter: infected women were no more likely to have a cat, clean a litter box, or have an outdoor-hunting cat. The exception: one US study found owning three or more kittens (not cats) was associated with higher infection rates.

Surprisingly, gardening is the real toxoplasmosis risk. The same European study found a strong association between working with soil and infection — because animals (including cats) use outdoor soil as a litter box. If you garden while pregnant, wear gloves and consider a mask.

Hair Dye

High doses of hair dye chemicals cause birth defects in rats — but those studies involved directly injecting pregnant rats with large quantities daily, which bears no resemblance to topical application 3–4 times over 9 months. Human studies have generally shown no association with birth defects. One Swedish study of hairdressers found slightly lower birth weights, but this likely reflected the job (standing all day) rather than the dye. Multiple detailed reviews conclude hair dye is safe during pregnancy. Even ACOG says it's fine after the first trimester.

Hot Tubs, Baths & Hot Yoga

This one has real teeth. A 2011 study of ~18,000 births found associations between hot tub use and certain birth defects (gastroschisis and anencephaly). Other studies confirmed the link with neural tube defects, and animal research supports the mechanism: raising body temperature above ~101°F in the first trimester increases the risk of neural tube defects like spina bifida.

Hot tubs are typically ~105°F, as is Bikram yoga. But cooler versions (85–90°F) are fine. The concern is limited to the first trimester — after that, neural tube formation is complete. A Spanish study also found very hot days could prompt earlier labor in late pregnancy, so if it's 105°F out and you're 36 weeks pregnant, stay inside.

Sex & Air Travel

Sex during pregnancy is safe. The baby is inside a fluid-filled sac protected by the closed cervix. You might have minor cervical bleeding from increased sensitivity — that's normal. As you get bigger, you'll need to get creative with positions, but there's no medical restriction.

Flying is fine. One Chicago-to-Boston flight delivers about 1% of the recommended pregnancy radiation limit. Even the longest available international flight is only 15% of the limit — and the limit itself is set at 1/20th of the level where actual risk has been demonstrated. A study comparing women who flew during pregnancy to those who didn't found no difference in preterm birth, fetal loss, or NICU admission. The only people who should consider limiting flights are very frequent flyers or flight attendants, who could approach the conservative limit over many trips.
