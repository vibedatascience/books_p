Data-Driven Conception

When exactly can you get pregnant, how do you detect ovulation, and does going off the pill mess things up?

The Fertility Window

Most people spend years trying not to get pregnant, then expect it to happen immediately. In reality, there's a surprisingly narrow window each month. The average cycle is 28 days. Around day 14, the egg is released — ovulation — and lives only 1–2 days. Sperm, however, survive up to 5 days inside the reproductive tract. That makes the total fertile window about 6 days: from 5 days before ovulation through ovulation day itself.

A study of 200+ couples trying to conceive tracked daily sex timing and daily urine samples to pinpoint ovulation. By isolating women who had sex on only one day in the fertile window, researchers could determine the exact probability of conception by day. The results: the day of ovulation and the day before each have ~30% conception rates. Two days before drops to ~15%. After ovulation, it's 0% — the egg is gone.

Does Sex Frequency Matter?

Many OBs recommend sex every other day instead of every day, under the theory that "saving up" sperm increases concentration. The data doesn't support this. The same study compared predicted pregnancy rates for couples who had sex once, twice, or three times in the 6-day window — the rates were nearly identical. Frequency doesn't matter. The only thing that matters is hitting ovulation day or the day before. One caveat: very long abstinence (10+ days) can reduce sperm effectiveness, so don't go to the other extreme.

Coming Off Birth Control

If you've been using barrier methods (condoms, diaphragm), your cycle is unaffected. The pill is more complicated — your body needs time to restart its natural ovulation cycle. About 60% of women resume a normal cycle within the first month. ~90% are back to normal within a few months. In the worst case, it can take up to 9 months, but virtually everyone normalizes by then.

Importantly, duration of pill use doesn't matter. Women on the pill for 12+ years returned to normal in the same timeframe as short-term users. And once you do ovulate, having been on the pill doesn't reduce pregnancy rates — women off the pill were slightly less likely to conceive in the first 3 months, but equally likely within a year. IUDs take about a month longer than the pill on average, with 80–90% of women pregnant within one year.

Detecting Ovulation

Since timing is everything, you need to know when you ovulate. There are three common methods, all evaluated in a study where researchers verified actual ovulation day with ultrasound:

Temperature charting (BBT) is free. You take your temperature every morning before getting out of bed. A ~0.5°F jump tells you ovulation happened yesterday — useful for learning your pattern over months, but only confirms ovulation after the fact. It identified the exact ovulation day 30% of the time, and the day before another 30%.

Cervical mucus monitoring is also free. Unlike temperature, it tells you ovulation is coming — you look for stretchy, egg-white-like mucus. It hit the exact day about 50% of the time, with another ~25% hitting the day before. Takes practice.

Ovulation pee sticks (OPKs) cost ~$30–40/month and detect the LH hormone surge that triggers ovulation. In the study, they identified ovulation day 100% of the time. A separate study found a 23% pregnancy rate within 2 months for women given OPKs, versus 15% without.

The Bottom Line

“Timing matters! Pregnancy rates are high if you have sex on the day of ovulation or the day before, but fall rapidly away from that.”

“It can take up to 9 months to resume your normal menstrual cycle after going off the pill, but there are no long-term effects on fertility.”

“Higher-tech methods, such as ovulation pee sticks, are pricier but very accurate.”
