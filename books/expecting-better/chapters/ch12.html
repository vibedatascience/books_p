Working Out and Resting Up

Is exercise important, dangerous, or both — and what about that no-back-sleeping rule?

Exercise

A 2010 review of 12 randomized trials of exercise during pregnancy (mostly walking, water aerobics, cycling ~3x/week) found that women who exercised gained about 1.3 fewer pounds. That's statistically significant but small — consistent with what we know about exercise and weight loss generally.

A 2009 review of all randomized trials looking at other outcomes was "comprehensive but ultimately disappointing." Exercise showed no impact on preterm birth, gestational age, C-section rate, fetal growth, APGAR scores, or length of labor. But crucially, it also showed no downsides. Women who exercise tend to have lower-risk pregnancies, though this is likely because they're healthier to begin with.

What to avoid: contact sports and activities with high fall risk (skiing, rock climbing) — a fall could cause placental abruption. One study of Olympic-level athletes found decreased blood flow to the baby when heart rate exceeded 90% of maximum, but this doesn't apply to recreational exercisers. Sit-ups after 20 weeks are often prohibited due to blood flow concerns from lying flat, but most evidence suggests they're fine — you'd feel uncomfortable if it were a problem.

Kegels

Kegels (pelvic floor exercises) are the one exercise with clear, strong evidence of benefit. A randomized trial of 300 women in Taiwan assigned half to do Kegels (3 sets of 8 reps, twice daily — about 15–20 minutes total). Women in the Kegel group had significantly less urinary incontinence in late pregnancy and up to 6 months postpartum. A 2009 review found Kegel-doers were less than half as likely to experience urinary incontinence.

There may be labor benefits too: one small study found the Kegel group pushed for 40 minutes on average vs. 45 for the control group, with only 22% pushing for more than an hour vs. 37%.

Prenatal Yoga

The studies are small but positive. A Taiwan study of 90 women found yoga reduced late-pregnancy discomfort (from 43% to 38%). A Thailand study of 74 women found the yoga group reported lower labor pain and labor was 2.5 hours shorter. The mechanism isn't clear — flexibility, pelvic opening, relaxation — but the direction is consistently positive.

Sleep

Sleep aids: Unisom is a pregnancy Category B drug — widely used, no evidence of risk. Ambien has good safety data for occasional use, but one Taiwanese study found long-term prescriptions were associated with preterm and low-birth-weight babies.

Sleep position: You're told not to sleep on your back after 20 weeks — the concern is the uterus compressing a blood vessel. But a study that measured uterine blood flow while women lay on their backs found no bad impact. A few women became uncomfortable and felt better when they moved. A review article concluded: "Advising women to sleep exclusively on the left side is not practical and is irrelevant to the vast majority of patients."

However, one more recent study did find an association between back/right-side sleeping and stillbirth (about double the rate). The sample was small and tested many hypotheses, but the finding can't be fully dismissed. Sleeping on your left side is unlikely to hurt, so it's a reasonable default if you can manage it — but don't panic if you wake up on your back.

  • 1
    Exercise is fine but not mandatory. No proven benefits beyond slight weight control, but no downsides either. Continue what you're already doing.
  • 2
    Do Kegels. Strong evidence they cut urinary incontinence by more than half and may shorten pushing time. 15–20 min/day.
  • 3
    Back sleeping restrictions are probably overblown, but left-side sleeping is a reasonable precaution given one concerning study.
The Bottom Line

“General exercise during pregnancy is fine. Not exercising during pregnancy is also fine. By and large, you should feel comfortable continuing to do what you are already doing.”

“Kegels prevent urinary incontinence and quite possibly improve your pushing ability in labor. Do them.”

“Unisom is safe to take. Ambien is also probably safe, but the evidence is a bit more mixed.”
