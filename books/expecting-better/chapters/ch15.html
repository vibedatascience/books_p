High-Risk Pregnancy

A reference guide to the most common third-trimester complications — what they are, what happens, and whether they recur.

Why the Third Trimester Matters

Doctor visits increase to every two weeks (and then weekly) during the third trimester for a reason: this is when complications most commonly emerge. Modern medicine has made enormous progress — conditions like Rh incompatibility, which caused infant anemia, heart failure, and death as recently as 1960, are now treated with a simple shot at 28 weeks that most women barely notice.

If you develop a high-risk condition, your doctor will be your primary resource. Treatments are highly individual. What follows is a brief reference — enough to understand a diagnosis, know what's at stake, and ask informed questions.

Common Conditions

Placenta previa occurs when the placenta partially or fully covers the cervix. It can cause vaginal bleeding and may require a Cesarean delivery, but the vast majority of cases resolve on their own. If it persists to term, a C-section is typically scheduled around 36–37 weeks.

Placental abruption is when the placenta detaches from the uterine wall. This causes painful contractions, bleeding, and risks preterm birth. If it happens at full term, the treatment is delivery. If preterm, management varies with the severity.

Gestational diabetes is diabetes diagnosed during pregnancy. It can produce a very large baby, increasing risks of C-section, shoulder problems during delivery, and metabolic issues in the newborn. It's managed through glucose monitoring, diet and exercise modification, and sometimes medication.

Preeclampsia involves high blood pressure and protein in the urine after 20 weeks. Symptoms may include headache, visual disturbances, and sudden weight gain. It can progress to eclampsia (seizures) or HELLP syndrome (destruction of red blood cells, liver dysfunction, low platelets). Untreated, it can be fatal for mother or baby. The only cure is delivery, which may need to happen preterm in severe cases. Magnesium sulfate is used to prevent seizures.

Cervical insufficiency is painless dilation of the cervix that can cause second-trimester miscarriage or very preterm birth. It's treated with progesterone or a cerclage — a stitch placed in the cervix to hold it closed. If it happens in one pregnancy, it will almost certainly happen again.

Fetal growth restriction means the baby is small and not reaching growth potential, possibly due to smoking, malnutrition, or placental problems. It carries risks of very low birth weight, preterm birth, and stillbirth. Management involves continual monitoring of fetal growth and blood flow, with early delivery when the baby would be better off outside than inside.

Placenta accreta occurs when the placenta invades too deeply into the uterine wall. Risk is higher with prior C-sections or placenta previa. It can cause massive hemorrhage at delivery and typically requires a C-section followed immediately by hysterectomy.

Will It Happen Again?

Unfortunately, most of these complications are more likely to recur in subsequent pregnancies. This is partly because observable risk factors (like obesity) persist, and partly because these conditions seem linked to genetic or physiological traits. Cervical insufficiency is essentially certain to recur. Preeclampsia risk is elevated but not guaranteed. Your doctor can help you plan accordingly.

The Bottom Line

If you develop a high-risk condition, your doctor is your best resource. Treatments are specific to the individual case. The conditions above are a starting point — enough to understand a diagnosis and ask informed questions.
