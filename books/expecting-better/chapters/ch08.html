Prenatal Screening & Testing

The major first-trimester medical decision — noninvasive screening vs. invasive testing, and what the numbers actually mean.

The Goal

All prenatal screening and testing aims to detect chromosomal abnormalities — most commonly Down syndrome (three copies of chromosome 21). Risk depends heavily on age. At 25, the chance is about 1 in 1,100. At 31, about 1 in 700. At 35, 1 in 374. At 40, 1 in 106. Two rarer conditions — Edward's syndrome (trisomy 18) and Patau syndrome (trisomy 13) — are more severe and typically fatal within the first year.

You have three options: (1) do nothing and learn at birth; (2) noninvasive screening (ultrasound + blood test, no risk to baby, but not definitive); or (3) invasive testing (CVS or amniocentesis — definitive answer, but carries a small miscarriage risk).

Noninvasive Screening

The first-trimester screen measures fluid behind the baby's neck (nuchal translucency) via ultrasound and two hormones in Mom's blood (PAPP-A and HCG). Down syndrome fetuses tend to have more neck fluid and different hormone levels. Some doctors also look for a nasal bone on ultrasound — its absence is another signal.

A large English study of ~57,000 pregnancies found this screening detects about 90% of Down syndrome cases overall, with a 6.3% false positive rate. But both numbers vary significantly with age. For women 20–24: 82% detection, 3% false positive. For women 40: 96% detection, but a 25% false positive rate. For women 45: 98% detection with a 50% false positive rate.

After a good screening result, a 31-year-old's risk drops from ~1 in 700 to roughly 1 in 6,000. Second-trimester screening (additional blood tests at 15–18 weeks) can push the combined detection rate to 97%.

Invasive Testing: CVS vs. Amnio

CVS (chorionic villus sampling) takes cells from the placenta at 10–12 weeks. Amniocentesis extracts amniotic fluid at 16–20 weeks. Both give a definitive answer — they sequence the baby's actual DNA. The commonly cited miscarriage risk of 1 in 200 is based on a 1970s study that didn't actually show a statistically significant risk increase.

Modern data paints a very different picture. The FASTER trial compared ~35,000 women who had amniocentesis to those who didn't — pregnancy loss was 1.0% vs 0.94%, a difference of about 1 in 1,600 and not statistically significant. A study of 5,000 CVS patients vs. 5,000 controls found lower miscarriage in the CVS group (2.7% vs 3.3%). Multiple recent studies suggest the actual procedure-related risk is roughly 1 in 800 at most — and possibly zero.

CVS and amniocentesis now carry indistinguishable risk, making CVS the clear winner since it can be done 6–10 weeks earlier. Modern ultrasound guidance has virtually eliminated the old risks of hitting the fetus or placenta.

Making the Decision

The old rule — invasive testing only for women over 35 — is based on flawed logic: it assumes a miscarriage is exactly as bad as having a child with a chromosomal problem. That's a deeply personal judgment that varies by person. A 31-year-old with a good screening result (risk ~1 in 6,000) faces a CVS miscarriage risk of ~1 in 800. The question becomes: would an unexpected chromosomal problem be more than 8 times worse for you than a miscarriage? There's no universal answer.

NIPT (cell-free fetal DNA testing) is a newer option that analyzes fetal DNA circulating in Mom's blood. Early studies show 98–99% detection with only a 0.5% false positive rate — far better than traditional screening. It's likely to become the standard, though as of 2013 it hadn't been tested in low-risk women or twin pregnancies.
