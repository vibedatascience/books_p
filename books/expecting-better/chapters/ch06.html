Beware of Deli Meats!

Which food restrictions actually matter — and which are overblown?

Not All Bacteria Are Equal

The pregnancy food restriction list is long: raw eggs, sushi, deli meats, rare steak, soft cheese, smoked fish. But these foods aren't all restricted for the same reason, and the risks aren't equal. There are five food-borne pathogens behind the list: salmonella, E. coli, campylobacter, Listeria, and toxoplasmosis. The key insight is that three of the five are no worse during pregnancy than at any other time.

Salmonella, E. coli, and campylobacter cause standard stomach-flu symptoms: diarrhea, nausea, vomiting. Unpleasant, but these infections are not especially more likely during pregnancy, nor do they typically affect the fetus directly. Normal food-safety caution is sufficient — no need to completely eliminate sushi or runny eggs from your diet if you'd normally eat them. The same precautions you take when not pregnant apply.

Toxoplasmosis — Harmful but Avoidable

Toxoplasmosis is caused by a parasite (toxoplasma gondii) — the reason pregnant women are told to avoid cat litter. But you're more likely to get it from undercooked meat or unwashed vegetables. In non-pregnant people, it causes mild flu-like symptoms or none at all. The danger in pregnancy is that you might not notice the infection, and if untreated, the parasite can reach the fetus.

Congenital toxoplasmosis affects about 1 in 1,500 babies, with complications including developmental delays, blindness, and epilepsy. However, avoiding it is straightforward: a European study found that roughly half of cases could be prevented by not eating raw or undercooked meat, and another 10% by washing vegetables and fruits thoroughly. About 25% of Americans already carry the parasite from past infection (from cats, gardening, or undercooked meat) and are immune — your doctor can test for this.

Listeria — Harmful and Hard to Avoid

Listeria is the real concern. Pregnant women are much more susceptible — up to a third of all Listeria infections are in pregnant women. If infected, miscarriage, preterm birth, or stillbirth occur in 10–50% of cases. Thankfully, it's rare: about 1 in 8,000 pregnancies.

The problem is that Listeria outbreaks are unpredictable. The last major outbreaks were in celery (2010), cantaloupes (2011, 29 deaths), and sprouts (2008). The last confirmed outbreak from deli meats was in 2005. Of outbreaks the CDC could trace between 1998 and 2008, 17% came from queso fresco (Mexican-style soft cheese from unpasteurized milk) and 10% from deli turkey. Between 2000 and 2008, the CDC could identify sources for only 262 of an estimated 24,000 cases — the incubation period can be a month or longer, making it nearly impossible to trace.

The author's calculation: avoiding ham sandwiches would lower the risk of Listeria from 1 in 8,333 to 1 in 8,255. She avoided queso fresco and deli turkey but kept other deli meats.

Mercury & Fish — It's Complicated

Large, long-lived fish (tuna, swordfish, shark) accumulate mercury, which is toxic to the developing fetal brain. Harvard researchers estimated that the difference in mercury between the average American woman and the most exposed woman produces about a 3.5 IQ-point difference in their children. Dropping from average mercury to zero would gain about 1 IQ point.

But fish also contain omega-3 fatty acids (DHA), which are great for brain development. Increasing DHA intake by 1 gram/day raises child IQ by an estimated 1.3 points. One serving of salmon has ~1.5g of DHA. Women who eat more fish have smarter children on average, even accounting for mercury exposure.

The best strategy: pick fish that are high in omega-3s and low in mercury. Salmon, sardines, and herring are ideal — high DHA, low mercury. Orange roughy and canned light tuna are the worst — low DHA, high mercury. Swordfish and sushi-grade tuna are in between — high mercury but also high DHA, making them better than low-DHA options.

The Revised Off-Limits List

What to actually avoid vs. standard advice

Source: Author's analysis of outbreak data — as cited in Expecting Better

  • 1
    Sushi and raw eggs are fine with normal caution. The bacteria they carry (salmonella, campylobacter) are no worse during pregnancy than at other times.
  • 2
    Avoid raw/rare meat (toxoplasmosis risk) and queso fresco (Listeria risk). Wash vegetables thoroughly.
  • 3
    Eat more fish, not less — but pick smart. Salmon, sardines, and herring give you high omega-3s with low mercury. Avoid orange roughy and limit canned tuna.
  • 4
    If you get sick, call your doctor immediately. For both Listeria and toxoplasmosis, early treatment reduces the chance of transmission to the baby.
The Bottom Line

“Don't worry too much about sushi and raw eggs — they might carry bacteria, but these bacteria are no worse when you are pregnant than when you are not.”

“The most dangerous food-borne bacteria is Listeria. Unfortunately, a lot of sources of outbreaks are random: cantaloupes, celery, sprouts.”

“Mercury is bad for your baby. Omega-3 fatty acids are good for your baby. Fish contain both. Your best option is to try to pick fish with a lot of omega-3s and not a lot of mercury.”
