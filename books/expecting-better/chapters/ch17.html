To Induce or Not to Induce?

Why the author avoided elective induction, how fetal monitoring can lead to unnecessary interventions, and what “natural” induction methods actually work.

Medical Induction

Labor induction has become routine: fewer than 10% of US births involved induction in 1990; by 2008, it was 25%. Induction is done with Pitocin (synthetic oxytocin) if the cervix is ready, or with a prostaglandin drug or balloon catheter to soften the cervix first. Regardless of method, induction is very likely to end with a baby.

The downsides: Pitocin may increase pain during labor — women who are induced are more likely to request an epidural. There is also direct and indirect evidence that induction can increase the risk of C-section, particularly when Pitocin is used alone. Pre-due-date induction is even more concerning. Babies who arrive on their own at 37 weeks do fine, but among induced births, those at 37 weeks do worse than those at 40.

Fetal Monitoring Pitfalls

Amniotic fluid: Low fluid (oligohydramnios) can signal a problem, but isolated low readings — where everything else looks normal — often don't warrant immediate induction. A small randomized study of 54 women found no difference in outcomes between women induced for isolated low fluid and those managed expectantly. Two easy ways to avoid false positives: stay very hydrated (drinking 2 liters of water before an ultrasound dramatically increases fluid readings) and ask your doctor to measure the “deepest vertical pocket” rather than total fluid volume, which is more prone to false alarms.

Non-stress test (NST): This monitors fetal heart rate over ~20 minutes. About 30% of babies “fail” because they're asleep. In a study of 485 women, 143 initially failed. Researchers then clapped loudly on the abdomen — 92% of the sleeping babies woke up and passed. Sugar, despite being commonly suggested, is completely ineffective. Clapping works. If the baby still doesn't respond after clapping and oxygen, it may be a true emergency.

  • 1
    Elective induction increases pain and possibly C-section risk. If you have a choice, going into labor on your own is the best option.
  • 2
    Drink 2 liters of water before fluid checks and ask for the deepest-vertical-pocket measurement to avoid unnecessary induction.
  • 3
    If the baby fails an NST, try clapping. 92% of “failed” tests resolved with this simple intervention.
DIY “Induction”

Between medical induction and doing nothing, there's a landscape of home remedies. The evidence on most is thin. Red raspberry leaf tea — no impact. Evening primrose oil — one study showed no benefit and possibly increased complications. Sex — semen contains a cervix-softening chemical, but a randomized trial showed no effect on labor onset. Acupuncture — mixed evidence; recent sham-controlled trials found no difference.

Two methods actually work. Nipple stimulation causes uterine contractions. In four randomized trials, 37% of women doing breast stimulation were in labor within 3 days, vs. only 6% without. The catch: it takes at least an hour a day for 3 days. Membrane stripping (done by your doctor during a cervical check) detaches the membranes from the uterine wall. Women who had this done were 25% more likely to go into labor within 2 days. It's painful, but has no increase in C-section rates.

The Bottom Line

“Best option: go into labor on your own.”

“Fluid monitoring. Two easy ways to avoid false positives: (1) stay hydrated, and (2) ask your doctor to measure the deepest vertical pocket rather than total fluid volume.”

“Non-stress test. Advice: just keep clapping.”

“Tea, oil, sex — all duds at starting labor. Nipple stimulation works, and so does membrane stripping.”
